HEALTH

Effect of Medical technology

Scroll Ahead

With the advancement of technology, the health sector has been revolutionized. It has allowed professionals to find new solutions to health problems and to save a lot of lives. But there are some problems that come up with this rapid technical implementations as well.

1. Improved Patient Care

Technology has led to the development of new medical devices, telemedicine, and electronic health records, which have improved patient outcomes and made healthcare more accessible. For example, telemedicine allows patients to receive medical care remotely, which is particularly beneficial for those who live in remote areas or have mobility issues.

2. Cost Reduction

The use of electronic health records has reduced administrative costs associated with paper records, while the development of new medical devices and treatments has improved efficiency and reduced the need for hospital stays. This has made healthcare more affordable and accessible to a wider range of people.

3. Enhanced Data Collection and Analysis

Electronic health records, wearable technology, and other digital tools have enabled healthcare providers to collect vast amounts of data about patients, which can be used to improve treatment plans, predict disease outbreaks, and track public health trends.

The adoption of electronic health records has increased from 9.4% in 2008 to 86.9% in 2017, according to the Office of the National Coordinator for Health Information Technology. EHRs have led to improved patient care, reduced medical errors, and cost savings.

The global telemedicine market is projected to reach $113.1 billion by 2025, growing at a compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 16.8%, according to a report by Grand View Research. Telemedicine has improved access to healthcare, particularly for patients in remote or underserved areas.

The global medical devices market is expected to reach $603.5 billion by 2026, growing at a CAGR of 5.4%, according to a report by Research and Markets. Medical devices have led to improved patient outcomes and reduced healthcare costs.

The global health apps market is expected to reach $111.1 billion by 2025, growing at a CAGR of 44.2%, according to a report by Allied Market Research. Health apps have led to increased patient engagement and improved disease management

HOWEVER

It's not all good health and happy life, there is a dark side to everything...

The increasing reliance on technology in healthcare has led to a dependence on technology that can sometimes hinder healthcare providers from delivering care. For example, if technology systems go down or malfunction, healthcare providers may be unable to access critical patient information or complete necessary tasks.

The use of electronic health records and other digital tools has raised concerns about privacy and security risks. Healthcare providers must implement strong security protocols to protect patient data from cyberattacks and data breaches.

The increasing use of technology in healthcare has led to job displacement, particularly in areas such as medical transcription and radiology. This has led to concerns about job security for healthcare workers.
